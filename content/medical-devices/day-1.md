# medical-devices — Day 1

## Q1: How would you approach designing the power management system for a battery-powered medical device that must operate continuously for 7 days?

**Answer:** I'd start by establishing a clear power budget. First, identify every active component in the system—microcontroller, sensors, wireless module, display, and any analog front-ends—and calculate their worst-case current draw across all operating modes (active, idle, sleep). For a 7-day runtime, I'd work backward from the battery capacity, applying derating factors for battery aging, temperature, and end-of-life voltage drop. I'd then architect a power tree with multiple voltage rails, using high-efficiency DC-DC converters for the main digital rail and low-dropout regulators (LDOs) for noise-sensitive analog sections. A key decision is selecting the battery chemistry—Li-ion is common for energy density, but requires protection circuitry (overcharge, over-discharge, short-circuit) per IEC 60601-1 requirements for medical safety. I'd also implement a fuel gauge IC for accurate remaining capacity estimation, and design the charging circuit to meet the device's ingress protection and sterilization requirements. Finally, I'd model the system's sleep-mode current—often the dominant factor in long-duration devices—and ensure the microcontroller can enter deep sleep while maintaining wake-on-interrupt for sensor events.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle the trade-off between using a switching regulator (efficiency) versus an LDO (noise) for an analog sensor supply? What battery protection features are mandatory for a Class II medical device?

## Q2: You're debugging a prototype medical device that intermittently resets during IEC 60601 ESD testing. How would you approach this?

**Answer:** Intermittent resets during ESD are often caused by the discharge current coupling into the reset circuitry or the power supply. I'd start by replicating the failure in a controlled ESD test setup, noting the polarity, voltage level, and contact point that triggers the reset. Then I'd use a systematic approach: first, examine the PCB layout around the reset pin and any exposed traces—ESD can couple into long, unguarded traces. I'd check if the reset line has a dedicated ESD protection diode or TVS diode close to the connector or button. Next, I'd look at the power supply: a momentary voltage drop from ESD coupling into the input can cause a brown-out reset. I'd verify the power supply decoupling capacitor values and placement, and check if the reset controller's threshold is properly set. I'd also examine the ground plane—a poor ground return path can allow ESD current to flow through sensitive logic. If the microcontroller has an internal brown-out reset (BOR) circuit, I'd verify its threshold and hysteresis settings. A common fix is adding a small series resistor (e.g., 100Ω–1kΩ) on the reset line close to the MCU, plus a 0.1µF capacitor to ground to filter transients. I'd also ensure all exposed metal (enclosures, connectors) has a low-impedance path to chassis ground.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you distinguish between a power-supply dip and a direct coupling event on the reset line? What ESD protection topology would you recommend for a USB port on a medical device?

## Q3: How would you structure a risk management file for a new Class II medical device per ISO 14971?

**Answer:** I'd begin with the intended use and reasonably foreseeable misuse of the device, as this defines the scope of the risk analysis. Then I'd create a hazard identification table, systematically listing hazards across categories: electrical (shock, fire), mechanical (moving parts, sharp edges), biological (biocompatibility, sterilization), radiation (if applicable), and usability (user error, misinterpretation of alarms). For each hazard, I'd document the hazardous situation and potential harm. Next, I'd estimate the severity of harm and probability of occurrence—this can be qualitative (e.g., using a risk matrix with categories like "negligible," "minor," "serious," "critical") or semi-quantitative. For each identified risk, I'd propose risk control measures, following the hierarchy: inherent safety by design (e.g., redundant sensors, fail-safe states), protective measures (e.g., fuses, enclosures, alarms), and information for safety (e.g., labeling, instructions). After implementing controls, I'd re-evaluate the residual risk and document whether it's acceptable per the company's risk acceptability criteria. The entire process is captured in a risk management file that includes the risk management plan, risk analysis, risk evaluation, risk control, and post-production surveillance plan. I'd also ensure traceability between hazards, risk controls, and verification/validation activities—for example, linking a shock hazard to the dielectric strength test in the test protocol.

**Possible follow-ups:** How do you handle risks that cannot be fully mitigated—when is residual risk considered acceptable? How would you integrate DFMEA with the ISO 14971 process?

## Q4: A medical device you're developing fails its conducted emissions test at a specific frequency during EMC pre-compliance. How would you diagnose and resolve this?

**Answer:** First, I'd confirm the failing frequency and its harmonics—this often points to a specific clock or switching frequency in the design. I'd review the schematic and PCB layout to identify potential sources: the main microcontroller clock, any DC-DC converter switching frequency, or a periodic sensor readout cycle. For a switching regulator, I'd check if the switching frequency or its harmonics align with the failing band. I'd then examine the PCB layout for long traces acting as antennas, poor return current paths, or inadequate decoupling. A common fix is to add ferrite beads on power inputs to the noisy section, or to adjust the switching regulator's slew rate if it has a dV/dt control pin. I'd also check the input filter design—a pi-filter (capacitor-inductor-capacitor) on the power input can be tuned to attenuate the specific frequency. If the noise is common-mode, I'd look at the cable shielding and grounding strategy; adding a common-mode choke on the cable might help. I'd also verify that the PCB stack-up has proper ground planes and that sensitive traces are not routed over split planes. In parallel, I'd simulate the noise path using a spectrum analyzer and near-field probe to physically locate the radiating element on the board. The fix might be as simple as moving a decoupling capacitor closer to the IC, or as involved as redesigning the input filter.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you decide between adding filtering versus redesigning the layout? What's the role of the ground plane in conducted emissions performance?

## Q5: You're leading a cross-functional team through a design review for a medical device that monitors multiple physiological parameters. A firmware engineer argues that a software-based calibration routine is sufficient, while a hardware engineer insists on a dedicated reference voltage circuit. How would you handle this disagreement?

**Answer:** I'd treat this as a risk-based decision, not a matter of opinion. First, I'd ask both engineers to articulate the specific requirements: what accuracy and stability does the measurement need over temperature, time, and supply voltage? I'd then have them present the quantitative trade-offs. For the hardware approach, I'd want to see the drift specifications of the reference voltage IC, its temperature coefficient, and the cost/board-space impact. For the software approach, I'd ask about the calibration algorithm's ability to compensate for drift, the frequency of recalibration needed, and whether the system has a known stable reference point (e.g., a precision resistor or a known physiological baseline). I'd also consider the regulatory implications: a hardware reference is typically easier to validate and document for IEC 60601 because it's a fixed, measurable component. A software calibration might introduce more variables and require more extensive verification testing. I'd then propose a structured decision: if the accuracy requirement is tight (e.g., ±1% over 0–50°C), a dedicated reference is likely the safer path. If the requirement is looser and the microcontroller has an internal bandgap reference with known drift, software calibration might suffice. I'd also suggest a compromise—perhaps a lower-cost reference IC with software trim to meet the spec. Ultimately, I'd document the decision rationale in the design history file, including the risk assessment and the verification plan for whichever approach we choose.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you verify that the chosen approach meets the accuracy specification during design verification? What documentation would you expect in the DHF for this decision?
