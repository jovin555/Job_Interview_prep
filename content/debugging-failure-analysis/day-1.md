# debugging-failure-analysis — Day 1

## Q1: How would you approach debugging an intermittent failure in a battery-powered medical sensor device that only occurs after several hours of continuous operation?
**Answer:** Intermittent failures that appear only after extended operation typically point to thermal effects, power supply drift, or cumulative software state issues. I would start by instrumenting the device to capture as much data as possible during the failure window — logging key voltages (battery, regulated rails, sensor reference), temperature at multiple board locations, and firmware state variables at high resolution. If the device has a real-time clock or watchdog timer, I'd check whether the failure correlates with specific time-based events in firmware. Next, I'd attempt to accelerate the failure in a controlled lab environment: heating the board with a thermal chamber or heat gun while monitoring, or cycling the battery through charge/discharge to reproduce the voltage sag that might trigger the fault. If thermal expansion is suspected, a freeze spray test can identify cold-sensitive joints or components. For firmware-related intermittents, I'd add a circular buffer that captures the last N seconds of sensor readings and state transitions before a reset or fault, then examine the pattern. The key principle is to narrow the search space by finding what changes between the working and failing states — temperature, voltage, time, or accumulated data — rather than randomly probing.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you distinguish between a hardware and a firmware root cause in such a scenario? What if the failure cannot be reproduced in the lab at all?

## Q2: Walk me through your process for conducting a root-cause investigation on a medical device that failed IEC 60601-1-2 EMC radiated emissions testing.
**Answer:** First, I would review the test setup and results carefully — which frequencies failed, by how much, and under what operating modes of the device. Emissions failures often trace to a few common sources: switching regulators, clock oscillators, high-speed digital buses, or poor grounding/return paths. I'd start by examining the PCB layout for the identified frequency and its harmonics — looking for long unbroken traces acting as antennas, inadequate decoupling near the switching components, or ground plane splits that force return currents through large loops. In the lab, I'd use a near-field probe to scan the board while it's operating, locating the strongest emitters at the failing frequencies. Once identified, I'd try mitigation techniques one at a time: adding ferrite beads on the offending trace, improving local decoupling capacitance, changing the switching regulator slew rate if adjustable, or adding shielding over the noisy section. Each change gets tested individually so I know what actually helped. If the emissions are from a known clock frequency, spreading the clock spectrum (if the application allows) or re-routing the trace closer to a ground plane can help. The goal is to find the minimum effective fix that doesn't compromise other aspects of the design — adding too much filtering can affect signal integrity or power efficiency.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you balance EMC fixes against the risk of introducing new problems in a Class II medical device? What if the emissions are coming from a component you cannot change (e.g., a certified wireless module)?

## Q3: A prototype board powers up but draws significantly more current than expected, and one voltage rail is 0.3V below its target. How would you systematically debug this?
**Answer:** I would approach this in stages, starting with the least invasive checks. First, I'd visually inspect the board under magnification for solder bridges, tombstoned components, or incorrect part values — especially around the power supply section. Then I'd measure resistance to ground on the low rail before applying power, to rule out a short. If that's clean, I'd power the board from a bench supply with current limiting set just above expected draw, and use a thermal camera or fingertip to find hot components — excessive current usually manifests as heat somewhere. I'd check the power supply feedback network: if it's a switching regulator, the feedback resistor divider might have the wrong ratio, or the output capacitor might be missing or damaged. For a linear regulator, the dropout voltage might be too high for the input voltage. I'd also verify that all decoupling capacitors are present and correctly oriented (polarized caps installed backwards will draw high current and heat up). If the rail powers downstream ICs, I'd isolate the rail by lifting one end of a series inductor or cutting a trace jumper, then re-measure — if the rail comes up to voltage with no load, the problem is in one of the downstream devices. Systematic isolation is faster than guessing.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the rail voltage is correct with no load but drops only when a specific IC is connected? How would you determine whether the IC is faulty or the PCB routing is the issue?

## Q4: You're debugging a communication failure between a microcontroller and a sensor over I2C — the bus sometimes hangs, and the sensor stops responding until the system is power-cycled. How would you diagnose this?
**Answer:** I2C hangs are often caused by the bus being held low by one device — typically the slave not releasing SDA or SCL after a transaction. I'd start by connecting an oscilloscope to both SDA and SCL lines and triggering on the hang condition. I'd look at the waveform: is the bus stuck low on SDA or SCL? Which device is driving it low at that moment? If the master (MCU) is driving, the firmware might be stuck in a state where it's waiting for an acknowledgment that never comes. If the slave is holding the line, it might have entered an undefined state due to a glitch, noise, or an incomplete transaction. I'd check the pull-up resistor values — too weak (high resistance) can cause slow rise times that violate timing, especially with bus capacitance from long traces or multiple devices. Too strong (low resistance) can cause excessive current that damages outputs. I'd also verify that the I2C timing in firmware matches the sensor's datasheet — a clock stretch that's too long or a start/stop condition that's improperly timed can confuse the slave. A common fix is to implement a bus recovery routine in firmware: toggle SCL nine times while SDA is high to reset stuck slaves, then reinitialize the bus. If the problem is noise-related, adding series resistors on SDA/SCL or reducing the bus speed can help.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you decide between a hardware fix (e.g., changing pull-ups) and a firmware workaround (e.g., bus recovery routine)? What if the problem only occurs when another subsystem (like a motor or pump) is active?

## Q5: A colleague on your team has been debugging a hardware issue for two days without progress, and the project schedule is tight. How would you approach helping them while maintaining team morale?
**Answer:** I'd start by asking them to walk me through what they've tried and what they've observed, without interrupting or jumping to conclusions. Often, the act of explaining the problem to someone else reveals assumptions or missed steps. I'd look for gaps in their approach — did they isolate the problem to a specific section of the circuit? Did they check the power supply first? Did they compare measurements against the schematic and layout? I'd suggest we go to the lab together and reproduce the issue, watching the measurements in real time. Sometimes a fresh pair of eyes spots something obvious, like a probe ground lead that's too long causing noise, or a multimeter set to the wrong range. If we still can't find it, I'd propose a structured divide-and-conquer approach: break the circuit into functional blocks and verify each one independently, starting with the power supply. I'd also check whether similar issues have been documented in the company's bug database or component errata. Throughout, I'd be careful to frame suggestions as collaborative ("what if we try...") rather than critical ("you should have..."). The goal is to solve the problem together, not to assign blame. If we're truly stuck, I'd recommend escalating to a senior engineer or the component manufacturer's FAE with a clear summary of what we've ruled out.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if your colleague is defensive about their work and resists suggestions? How would you handle a situation where you discover they made a fundamental design error that caused the problem?
