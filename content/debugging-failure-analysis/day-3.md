# debugging-failure-analysis — Day 3

## Q1: How would you approach diagnosing a firmware crash that only occurs when a medical device is connected to a specific accessory cable, but never when using the test harness on the bench?

**Answer:** This sounds like a classic firmware-hardware interaction issue where the accessory cable introduces electrical characteristics that differ from the test harness. I'd start by characterizing the difference between the two cables — measuring their impedance, capacitance, and checking pin-to-pin continuity, looking for anything like a missing shield connection or different wire gauge that could affect signal integrity. Next, I'd scope the critical communication lines (UART, I2C, or whatever protocol the accessory uses) at the microcontroller pins while connected to each cable, looking for edge-rate degradation, excessive ringing, or noise coupling that could cause the firmware to misinterpret data or trigger unintended interrupts. I'd also check whether the cable introduces a different ground path or alters the power-up sequence — for example, if the accessory cable connects a power pin before ground, it could create a latch-up condition or cause the microcontroller to power through an I/O pin, leading to unpredictable behavior. If I find signal integrity issues, I'd add series termination resistors or adjust drive strength in firmware. If the issue is timing-related, I'd add debounce logic or edge-detection state machines to reject glitches. The key is to reproduce the failure reliably with the suspect cable, then methodically isolate which electrical parameter difference triggers the crash.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you modify your firmware to make it more robust against cable-induced noise without knowing the root cause yet? What if the crash is non-deterministic and takes hours to reproduce — how would you accelerate your debugging?

## Q2: Walk me through your approach to identifying the root cause of a thermal failure in a sealed medical device that was returned from the field with a melted plastic housing near the battery charging circuit.

**Answer:** I'd approach this as a structured failure analysis, starting with non-destructive inspection before any disassembly. First, I'd take high-resolution photographs and use a thermal camera if available to see if there are any residual thermal patterns. I'd also X-ray the device to look for internal damage without opening the sealed enclosure. Once documented, I'd carefully open the housing and look for visual evidence — discolored PCB areas, swollen capacitors, cracked solder joints, or signs of arcing. I'd measure the charging circuit components: check the battery charger IC for shorted pins, measure the inductor's DC resistance to see if it's partially shorted, and inspect the current-sense resistor for damage. I'd also check the thermal management features — did the thermal pad make proper contact with the housing? Was the thermistor reading correctly? Next, I'd recreate the failure scenario on a known-good unit by stress-testing the charging circuit under worst-case conditions: high ambient temperature, depleted battery drawing maximum charge current, and possibly a partially blocked heat path simulating the device being placed on a soft surface. I'd monitor component temperatures with thermocouples and compare against the manufacturer's derating curves. The root cause could be a component operating beyond its safe operating area, a design margin issue where the thermal protection threshold was set too high, or a manufacturing defect like a poorly seated thermal interface. I'd document findings in a formal report with corrective actions — which might include adding a hardware thermal cutoff, adjusting the firmware charge profile to reduce current at high temperatures, or improving the thermal interface design.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you determine whether the failure was a one-off manufacturing defect versus a systematic design margin issue? What accelerated life test would you design to validate your fix?

## Q3: How would you debug a situation where a medical device passes all functional tests at room temperature but fails consistently when placed in an environmental chamber at 40°C?

**Answer:** Temperature-dependent failures often point to component parameter drift, thermal expansion causing intermittent connections, or protection circuits tripping. I'd start by instrumenting the device with thermocouples at key locations — the main processor, voltage regulators, sensor interfaces, and any components near heat sources — while running it in the chamber. I'd monitor critical voltages, current consumption, and communication bus activity as the temperature ramps up, looking for the exact temperature at which the failure occurs. Common culprits include voltage regulators dropping out of regulation as their thermal shutdown or current limit activates, crystal oscillators drifting outside the microcontroller's acceptable range, or ADC references becoming unstable. I'd also check for thermal expansion issues — a BGA package might lose contact with a solder ball, or a connector might separate slightly. If the failure is consistent at a specific temperature threshold, I'd use freeze spray or a hot-air gun on individual components on a bench unit to isolate which component is temperature-sensitive. Once identified, I'd check the component's datasheet specifications against the operating conditions — sometimes a component rated for 85°C operation still has degraded performance at 40°C if other parameters like supply voltage or load current are at their limits. The fix might involve selecting a higher-temperature-rated component, adding thermal management (heat sinking, airflow), or adjusting firmware to reduce power consumption at elevated temperatures.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the failure only occurs during the temperature transition (ramping up or down) rather than at steady-state? How would you differentiate between a component that's genuinely out of spec versus one that's been damaged by prior thermal stress?

## Q4: How would you approach a signal integrity issue where a high-speed SPI bus between a microcontroller and an ADC shows intermittent bit errors, but only when the device's motor driver is active?

**Answer:** This is a classic EMI coupling problem where the motor driver's switching noise is coupling into the SPI bus. I'd start by confirming the noise source — scope the SPI lines (SCK, MOSI, MISO, and the chip select) while the motor is off, then while running at various speeds and loads. I'd look for noise on the signal traces that correlates with the motor's PWM switching edges. The coupling path could be conducted (shared power supply or ground bounce) or radiated (proximity of traces to motor cables). I'd first check the power supply rails with the motor running — if the 3.3V or 5V rail shows ripple at the PWM frequency, that noise could be coupling into the ADC's reference or the microcontroller's I/O buffers. I'd also probe ground at multiple points to check for ground bounce. For radiated coupling, I'd use a near-field probe to scan over the PCB while the motor runs, looking for areas with high field strength near the SPI traces. Mitigation strategies would include: adding ferrite beads on the motor power lines, increasing the physical separation between motor driver traces and SPI traces, adding series resistors on SPI lines to slow edge rates (if timing margin allows), implementing a ground pour or guard trace around the SPI bus, and potentially using differential signaling or a shielded SPI cable if the ADC is on a separate board. In firmware, I could add CRC checking on SPI transactions and retry on failure, or temporarily disable the motor during critical ADC reads if the application timing allows.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you determine whether the noise is coupling through the ground plane versus through the power supply? What if you can't change the PCB layout — what firmware or software mitigations would you consider?

## Q5: A field-returned medical device shows intermittent resets, and your analysis points to the watchdog timer expiring. The firmware team insists the code is correct and the watchdog timeout is set appropriately. How would you approach resolving this cross-team disagreement and finding the real root cause?

**Answer:** This is as much a collaboration and communication challenge as a technical one. I'd start by acknowledging that both teams have valid perspectives — the firmware team has confidence in their code, and the hardware team has data pointing to watchdog resets. Rather than assigning blame, I'd propose a joint debugging session where we instrument the device together to capture exactly what happens before the watchdog fires. I'd suggest adding a diagnostic firmware build that logs the program counter, stack trace, and key system state variables to non-volatile memory just before the watchdog reset occurs. On the hardware side, I'd add monitoring of the supply voltage rail with a high-speed scope triggered on the reset signal, looking for brownouts or glitches that could cause the firmware to hang or enter an unexpected state. I'd also check the watchdog's own supply and its connection to the microcontroller — a noisy or intermittent watchdog reset line could cause false resets that look like watchdog timeouts. If we still can't find it, I'd propose a controlled experiment: run the device with the watchdog disabled (for short periods in a safe configuration) and see if the system still resets, which would indicate a hardware reset source rather than a firmware hang. Throughout the process, I'd maintain a shared log of hypotheses tested and results, so both teams can see the evidence accumulate. The goal is to find the root cause together, not to prove one team right. If the issue turns out to be a subtle hardware-firmware interaction — like a noise glitch on an interrupt line causing the firmware to enter an infinite loop — that's a system-level fix that requires both teams to collaborate on the solution.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the firmware team is unwilling to modify their code for diagnostic logging because it's already passed code review? How would you handle a situation where the intermittent reset cannot be reproduced in the lab at all?
