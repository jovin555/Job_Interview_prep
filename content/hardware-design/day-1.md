# hardware-design — Day 1

## Q1: You reduced signal noise by 15% through PCB layout and decoupling changes at Company A. Walk through your decoupling strategy.
**Answer:** For high-accuracy sensor front-ends on Class II medical devices, I placed decoupling caps in a value ladder (e.g. 100nF + 1uF + 10uF) as close to the IC power pins as trace geometry allowed, with dedicated via-to-plane stitching rather than sharing vias with other nets. I also reviewed return path continuity — checking that signal traces didn't cross plane splits — since a broken return path was a bigger noise contributor than cap placement alone. The 15% noise reduction came from a combination of tighter decoupling placement and cleaning up a plane split that was forcing return currents through a long loop under a sensitive analog trace.
**Possible follow-ups:** How did you measure the 15% figure (bench setup, instrumentation)? What would you have done differently if space constraints prevented placing caps at the ideal distance?

## Q2: What's the difference between placing decoupling capacitors for a digital IC versus an analog front-end, and why does it matter more on a medical sensor board?
**Answer:** Digital ICs mainly need to supply fast transient switching current, so cap placement is about minimizing loop inductance to the pin. Analog front-ends care about that too, but additionally need the decoupling network to filter high-frequency noise out of the supply rail itself, since supply noise couples directly into the measurement. On the medical sensor boards I worked on, I used separate analog and digital ground/power domains with a single star-point connection, and put an extra ferrite bead + local low-ESR cap on the analog rail feeding the ADC/sensor front-end to keep digital switching noise from leaking in.
**Possible follow-ups:** How do you decide where the star-point connection goes on a mixed-signal board? Have you dealt with a case where ground domain separation caused more problems than it solved?

## Q3: How do you approach stack-up and layer assignment on a high-speed, mixed-signal medical device PCB?
**Answer:** I start by grouping signals by class — high-speed digital, sensitive analog, power — and assign each its own reference plane rather than sharing. On boards with tight layer budgets I've used a 6-layer stack with dedicated analog and digital ground planes and controlled-impedance layers for any high-speed digital or communication lines (I2C/SPI/UART tend to be forgiving, but anything approaching CAN-FD speeds gets impedance-controlled routing). I also keep high-speed digital routing on layers adjacent to a solid ground plane and route analog sense lines away from switching regulators and digital buses.
**Possible follow-ups:** How do you handle stack-up when layer count is cost-constrained? What impedance-control tolerance do you typically specify to your fab?

## Q4: Describe a power supply design decision you made that had to balance regulatory (IEC60601) requirements against performance or cost.
**Answer:** On the maternal/infant monitoring product line, patient-connected circuitry required reinforced isolation per IEC60601-1, which meant an isolated DC-DC stage plus isolated signal chain (isolation amplifiers/opto-isolated digital lines) rather than a simpler shared-ground design. That added BOM cost and board area, but was non-negotiable for patient safety classification. I worked with the isolation barrier creepage/clearance requirements early in layout planning rather than retrofitting them, since moving an isolation barrier after routing is expensive in both time and respin cost.
**Possible follow-ups:** How do you verify creepage/clearance compliance before sending a board to test? What's your process when a component you need doesn't have a datasheet isolation rating that clearly meets the standard?

## Q5: Tell me about a time a PCB design issue only showed up after fabrication or in testing — how did you find the root cause?
**Answer:** This is where 8D root cause process came in at Company A — for a critical medical device failure, I didn't jump to a layout fix immediately; I first reproduced the failure condition on the bench, isolated whether it was component-level, layout-level, or firmware-timing related, and used containment actions (e.g. flagging affected units) while the investigation was ongoing. The root cause traced back to a marginal decoupling/return-path issue that only manifested under a specific sensor loading condition not covered in early bench testing. The fix was a layout revision plus adding that loading condition to the test coverage so it wouldn't be missed on future revisions.
**Possible follow-ups:** How do you decide when a hardware issue needs a full 8D versus a quick fix? What test coverage gap did this reveal, and how did you close it long-term?
