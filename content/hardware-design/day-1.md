# hardware-design — Day 1

## Q1: How would you approach selecting a power management architecture for a battery-powered medical device that must operate continuously for several days?
**Answer:** I would start by establishing the system-level power budget: identify every rail voltage needed (e.g., 3.3V for digital, 5V for analog front-end, possibly a higher rail for actuators or wireless transmission), estimate peak and average current draws per rail, and determine acceptable ripple and noise levels—especially for analog sensor circuits where power supply rejection matters. For a medical device requiring multi-day continuous operation, efficiency across the full load range is critical, not just at peak load. I'd evaluate trade-offs between linear regulators (low noise, simple, but inefficient for large voltage drops) and switching converters (high efficiency but introduce switching noise that must be filtered). A typical approach is a hybrid: a switching preregulator to efficiently drop battery voltage to an intermediate rail, followed by low-dropout linear regulators on noise-sensitive analog rails. Battery chemistry (Li-ion vs. primary cells) dictates the voltage range and discharge curve, which affects converter topology—buck, boost, or buck-boost. I'd also include protection features: reverse polarity, overcurrent, undervoltage lockout to prevent deep discharge, and inrush current limiting. Finally, I'd simulate the design under worst-case load profiles and temperature extremes, then validate with thermal imaging and efficiency measurements on the prototype.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle the trade-off between efficiency and noise for a rail powering a high-resolution ADC? What battery monitoring or fuel-gauging approach would you recommend for a medical device?

## Q2: Walk me through your process for debugging a PCB that passes functional tests but intermittently fails under temperature cycling.
**Answer:** Intermittent temperature-related failures often point to coefficient of thermal expansion (CTE) mismatches, solder joint cracks, or component parametric drift. I'd start by characterizing the failure: log the exact temperature at which failures occur (both rising and falling), the specific function that fails, and whether the failure is repeatable. Then I'd narrow the search using a divide-and-conquer approach—monitor critical supply rails, clock signals, and data lines with an oscilloscope while cycling the board in a thermal chamber. If the failure is mechanical, I'd perform X-ray inspection on BGA and fine-pitch components, looking for head-in-pillow or voiding issues, and cross-section suspect joints. For electrical drift, I'd check components with known temperature coefficients—voltage references, oscillators, and precision resistors—by measuring their output versus temperature on a bench with a thermocouple probe and hot air gun. I'd also review the PCB stackup: mismatched CTE between the board material and large components (e.g., ceramic capacitors on FR4) can cause flexure and microcracks. If the root cause remains elusive, I'd instrument the board with thermocouples at multiple locations and run a controlled temperature ramp while logging all relevant signals with a data acquisition system, correlating the failure event with thermal gradients across the board.

**Possible follow-ups:** What specific design rules or layout practices would you change to prevent CTE-related failures in future revisions? How would you distinguish between a solder-joint issue and a component parametric drift?

## Q3: How would you design the analog front-end for a pressure sensor in a respiratory monitoring device, considering both accuracy and noise requirements?
**Answer:** I'd begin by understanding the sensor itself: its output type (resistive bridge, capacitive, or voltage output), sensitivity, excitation requirements, and the expected signal amplitude. For a typical resistive bridge pressure sensor in respiratory applications, the differential output might be on the order of millivolts per cmH₂O, with a bandwidth of 0–10 Hz for breathing waveforms. The key challenge is extracting this small signal from noise—both from the sensor and from the system. I'd use a differential instrumentation amplifier with high common-mode rejection ratio (CMRR) as the first gain stage, placed as close to the sensor as possible to minimize noise pickup on traces. The gain should be set to match the ADC's input range without saturating on the maximum expected pressure. I'd include a low-pass anti-aliasing filter before the ADC, with a cutoff around 20–30 Hz to pass respiratory signals while rejecting line-frequency noise and higher-frequency interference. For the sensor excitation, I'd use a precision voltage reference rather than the supply rail, and if it's a resistive bridge, I'd implement a ratiometric measurement where the ADC reference is derived from the same excitation voltage, canceling out drift. Layout is critical: guard rings around the high-impedance inputs, separate analog and digital ground planes with a single-point connection, and careful routing to keep switching converter noise away from the analog section. I'd also include a calibration routine in firmware to correct for offset and gain errors using known pressure references during manufacturing.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle baseline drift over temperature or time? What if the sensor requires AC excitation to avoid polarization effects?

## Q4: Describe your approach to reviewing a PCB layout for signal integrity and EMC compliance before sending it to fabrication.
**Answer:** I follow a structured checklist organized by risk priority. First, I verify the stackup: ensure continuous reference planes under high-speed traces, confirm that impedance-controlled traces have the correct width and spacing per the stackup calculation, and check that no trace crosses a split in its reference plane. For critical signals—clocks, high-speed data lines, and sensitive analog traces—I check length matching for parallel buses, proper termination (series, parallel, or AC depending on topology), and that return current paths are short and uninterrupted. I look at decoupling: each IC should have a low-ESR capacitor within a few millimeters of each power pin, with vias placed to minimize loop area between the capacitor, the IC, and the power plane. For EMC, I examine the board edges: no traces running parallel to the edge without a ground guard trace, and I/O filtering should be placed right at the connector with a low-impedance path to chassis ground. I check that high-frequency switching nodes (e.g., buck converter switch nodes) are as small as possible and kept away from board edges and sensitive analog sections. I also review the grounding strategy: a single-point or star ground for mixed-signal sections, and ensure that high-current return paths don't share a common impedance with sensitive analog returns. Finally, I run a design rule check (DRC) for manufacturing constraints, and if the board has high-speed interfaces, I'd simulate critical nets for impedance and crosstalk using the layout tool's field solver.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you prioritize which signal integrity issues to fix when you have limited board area? What would make you decide to add a shielding can or ferrite bead versus fixing the layout?

## Q5: A colleague proposes using a new, smaller package for a voltage regulator to save board space in a medical device. How would you evaluate the risks and decide whether to approve the change?
**Answer:** I'd approach this as a structured risk assessment, not a simple yes/no. First, I'd compare the thermal performance: smaller packages typically have higher thermal resistance (θJA), so I'd calculate the junction temperature at the device's maximum load and worst-case ambient temperature, including any derating required for medical safety margins. If the junction temperature exceeds the manufacturer's recommended limit, the change is unacceptable without heatsinking or airflow. Next, I'd check the electrical specifications: does the smaller package have the same or equivalent power rating, dropout voltage, quiescent current, and transient response? Sometimes smaller packages trade off current capability or have different internal compensation. I'd also consider manufacturability: is the package type (e.g., QFN vs. DFN vs. BGA) compatible with the existing assembly process, and does it require different stencil design or reflow profile? For a medical device, reliability data matters—I'd look for the manufacturer's qualification reports (e.g., AEC-Q100 or similar) and check if the package has known failure modes like solder joint cracking under thermal cycling. I'd also evaluate the impact on PCB layout: smaller packages may require tighter clearances, which could affect signal integrity or introduce coupling to adjacent traces. Finally, I'd prototype the change on a small batch of boards and run accelerated life testing (temperature cycling, vibration, and functional test) before approving it for production. If the risk is moderate but the space savings are significant, I might approve it with additional testing requirements rather than rejecting outright.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the smaller package is only available from a single supplier—how does that affect your decision? How would you document this change in the design history file for regulatory compliance?
