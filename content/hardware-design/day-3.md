# hardware-design — Day 3

## Q1: How would you approach selecting between a linear charger and a switching charger for a Li-ion battery in a portable medical device that must minimize both heat and board area?

**Answer:** The core trade-off is between thermal efficiency and physical size versus noise and complexity. A linear charger is simpler, smaller in component count, and produces no switching noise — attractive for noise-sensitive analog front-ends. However, its efficiency is roughly (Vout/Vin), so with a 5V input charging a 3.7V cell, you're dissipating about 26% of the power as heat. For charge currents above ~500mA in a compact enclosure, that heat becomes difficult to manage without a heatsink or forced airflow, which may not be acceptable in a medical device.

A switching charger (buck topology) can achieve 85–95% efficiency, dramatically reducing heat. The penalty is the switching noise at the converter's frequency (typically 1–3 MHz) and its harmonics, plus the need for an inductor and larger input/output capacitors, increasing board area. For a medical device, I would evaluate whether the switching frequency and its harmonics fall within the passband of any sensitive measurement channels. If they do, I'd consider a linear charger with a lower charge current (accepting longer charge times) or a switching charger with post-filtering (an LC stage or LDO after the charger output) to clean up the supply rail. I'd also examine the battery charging profile: if the device spends most of its life on a charger at the top-off stage (low current), the linear charger's thermal penalty is much smaller, making it more viable.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you decide whether post-filtering a switching charger is sufficient, or whether you need to switch to a linear charger entirely? What specific measurements would you take to characterize the switching noise coupling into your analog circuitry?

## Q2: Walk me through your process for selecting a TVS diode for protecting a medical device's external connector that carries both power and a sensitive analog signal.

**Answer:** I'd start by defining the threat model: what standard am I protecting against — IEC 61000-4-2 (ESD), IEC 61000-4-5 (surge), or both? For a medical device with an external connector, I'd typically need to meet both. Then I'd determine the clamping voltage that the downstream circuitry can survive. For a sensitive analog signal, the TVS's capacitance becomes critical — a standard TVS might have 100–500 pF, which would load and distort the signal. I'd look for low-capacitance TVS diodes (under 5 pF) or a TVS array specifically designed for signal lines.

For the power line, capacitance is less of a concern, but I need to ensure the TVS's standoff voltage is above the normal operating voltage plus tolerance, and that its clamping voltage at the rated surge current stays below the absolute maximum rating of the downstream regulator or IC. I'd also check the peak pulse power rating: for a given surge waveform (e.g., 8/20 µs), the TVS must absorb the energy without failing short or open. Finally, I'd consider the layout — the TVS should be placed as close to the connector as possible, with a low-inductance path to the chassis ground or signal return, to minimize the voltage spike seen by the protected circuit. For the analog signal, I might add a series resistor before the TVS to limit current and reduce loading, trading off some signal amplitude for better protection.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you verify in the lab that your TVS selection is adequate? What failure mode would you worry about most for a TVS in a medical device, and how would you design around it?

## Q3: How would you design a reverse polarity protection circuit for a medical device that draws up to 2A peak, while minimizing voltage drop and ensuring no reverse leakage current?

**Answer:** The classic approach is a series diode (Schottky for low forward voltage), but at 2A even a Schottky drops ~0.4–0.5V, dissipating nearly 1W — that's significant heat and a voltage penalty that might affect a 3.3V rail's headroom. A better approach for a medical device is a P-channel MOSFET in a high-side configuration. The MOSFET's body diode is oriented to block reverse current when the supply is reversed, but when the supply is correct, the gate is pulled low (relative to source) through a resistor and zener clamp, turning the MOSFET fully on. The voltage drop is then just I*Rds(on), which can be under 10 mV for a suitable MOSFET, with negligible power loss.

Key design considerations: the gate-source voltage must stay within the MOSFET's rating — typically ±20V, so a zener diode (e.g., 12V) clamps Vgs. The gate pull-down resistor must be sized to turn the MOSFET on quickly enough for the load's inrush, but not so small that it wastes current. I'd also add a small capacitor across the gate-source to prevent the MOSFET from turning off during brief supply dips. For a medical device, I'd verify that the protection circuit doesn't interfere with ESD testing — the MOSFET's body diode can act as a clamp during reverse ESD events, which is usually beneficial. If the device must survive a sustained reverse voltage (e.g., a user connects the battery backwards for minutes), I'd ensure the MOSFET's body diode doesn't conduct significantly — it will block reverse current, but the gate drive circuit must not be damaged by the reverse voltage.

**Possible follow-ups:** What happens if the supply voltage is applied correctly but the MOSFET's gate drive fails short — how does the circuit behave? How would you test that the protection circuit doesn't introduce a single-point failure that compromises the device's safety?

## Q4: (Behavioral) Imagine you're in a design review for a medical device's sensor front-end, and the firmware lead argues that the ADC sampling rate can be reduced to save power, while you believe the lower rate will alias noise into the passband and degrade measurement accuracy. How would you handle this disagreement?

**Answer:** I'd first acknowledge the firmware lead's valid concern — power is a critical constraint in a battery-powered medical device, and reducing the sampling rate is a legitimate way to save it. Then I'd frame the discussion around data rather than opinion. I'd propose we run a quick analysis or simulation together: take the known noise spectrum of the analog front-end (including 50/60 Hz pickup, amplifier noise, and any switching converter ripple), and calculate what happens when we sample at the proposed lower rate versus the current rate. If the lower rate causes aliasing of out-of-band noise into the signal band, I'd show the resulting SNR degradation and explain why that might fail the accuracy requirements in the product specification.

If the firmware lead still disagrees, I'd suggest a practical experiment: capture a few minutes of real sensor data at the higher rate, then digitally decimate it to simulate the lower rate and compare the measurement accuracy. That gives us objective data to decide. If the lower rate truly works without aliasing (perhaps because the analog anti-aliasing filter is already aggressive enough), then I'd support the change. If it doesn't, we have evidence to justify keeping the higher rate or adding a steeper analog filter to allow the lower rate. Throughout, I'd keep the focus on meeting the product requirements — accuracy, power, and safety — rather than whose approach "wins."

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the firmware lead insists on the lower rate despite the data showing aliasing, citing a tight power budget? How would you escalate or compromise? How would you document this decision in the design history file?

## Q5: How would you approach characterizing the settling time of a multi-channel ADC's input multiplexer when switching between two sensor channels with very different source impedances?

**Answer:** The key concern is that the ADC's internal sampling capacitor must charge to the new voltage within the acquisition time, and the source impedance forms an RC time constant with that capacitor. If one channel has a high source impedance (e.g., a high-impedance sensor with no buffer) and the other is low, switching from the low-impedance channel to the high-impedance channel can leave the sampling capacitor incompletely charged, causing crosstalk errors.

My approach would be: first, calculate the worst-case RC time constant using the ADC's datasheet sampling capacitance and the maximum source impedance of the high-impedance channel. Compare that to the ADC's specified acquisition time — a rule of thumb is to allow at least 7–10 time constants for 12-bit accuracy. If the time constant exceeds the acquisition time, I know I need either a buffer (op-amp) on the high-impedance channel, or I must increase the acquisition time in the ADC's configuration.

For characterization, I'd set up a test: connect a known DC voltage to the low-impedance channel and a different DC voltage to the high-impedance channel. Program the ADC to switch between them, and capture the first few samples after switching. Plot the values — you should see an exponential approach to the correct value. The number of samples needed to settle to within 1 LSB tells you the effective settling time. I'd repeat this at different ADC clock speeds and with different acquisition time settings. If the settling is too slow, I'd add a unity-gain buffer (op-amp) on the high-impedance channel, which also provides a low-impedance drive to the ADC. The trade-off is added board space, cost, and a small offset error from the buffer.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you choose between adding an external buffer versus using the ADC's built-in PGA (if available) to handle the high-impedance channel? What if the high-impedance sensor is also very low amplitude — how does that change your approach?
