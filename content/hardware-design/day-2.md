# hardware-design — Day 2

## Q1: How would you approach selecting a power management architecture for a battery-powered medical device that must operate continuously for 7 days?
**Answer:** I'd start by building a detailed power budget from the worst-case current draw of every subsystem — sensor, microcontroller, wireless radio, display, and any analog front-ends. For a 7-day runtime, the average current consumption must be minimized, which typically means the microcontroller spends most of its time in deep sleep, waking only to take measurements or transmit data. I'd evaluate a buck-boost converter for the main rail to maintain regulation across the full Li-ion discharge curve (3.0–4.2 V), and use low-dropout regulators (LDOs) for noise-sensitive analog sections. Battery capacity is sized with a derating factor (typically 80% usable capacity for Li-ion) plus margin for end-of-life degradation. I'd also include a fuel gauge IC with coulomb counting for accurate remaining-capacity reporting, and a dedicated charger IC with JEITA-compliant temperature monitoring for safety. The trade-off is always between efficiency (switching regulators) and noise (LDOs) — for medical sensing, I'd likely use a hybrid approach: a buck-boost for the digital rail and post-regulated LDOs for the analog sensor supply.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle the transition between active and sleep modes to avoid inrush current glitches? What battery chemistry would you choose and why?

## Q2: Walk me through your process for debugging a new PCB that powers on but draws excessive quiescent current and runs hot.
**Answer:** I'd follow a systematic isolation approach. First, measure the total current at the power input and compare against the expected budget. Then use a thermal camera or probe by hand to identify which components are heating up — that immediately narrows the search. Next, I'd check for soldering defects: bridges on fine-pitch ICs, tombstoned passives, or incorrectly oriented diodes/polarized capacitors. I'd verify all power rail voltages at the IC pins with an oscilloscope, looking for ripple or droop that might indicate a shorted bypass capacitor or a damaged regulator. If a specific rail is drawing excessive current, I'd use a current probe or measure voltage drop across series resistors (if present) to isolate the offending branch. For medical devices, I'd also check that the power sequencing matches the datasheet requirements — some ICs can latch up or draw excess current if rails come up in the wrong order. If nothing obvious appears, I'd remove components one at a time from the suspect rail, starting with the largest bypass capacitors (which are common failure points), until the current drops.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you distinguish between a manufacturing defect and a design error? What if the board only exhibits the problem after 30 minutes of operation?

## Q3: How would you design the analog front-end for a pressure sensor used in a respiratory monitoring device, considering noise and accuracy requirements?
**Answer:** I'd start by characterizing the sensor itself — its output type (ratiometric voltage, current, or digital), sensitivity, bandwidth, and noise floor. For a medical-grade respiratory flow sensor, the signal bandwidth is typically DC to maybe 10–20 Hz, so I'd design an anti-aliasing filter with a cutoff around 30–50 Hz using a second-order Sallen-Key topology. The amplifier chain would use an instrumentation amplifier for its high common-mode rejection, followed by a gain stage to map the sensor's full-scale output into the ADC's input range. I'd pay careful attention to the reference voltage — using a low-noise precision reference rather than the supply rail — and route the analog traces with a dedicated ground plane return. For the PCB layout, I'd keep the analog section physically separated from the digital switching noise (microcontroller, wireless radio), use a star-ground or split-ground approach, and add ferrite beads on the supply lines to the analog section. I'd also include a test point for each stage so the signal chain can be probed during validation. Finally, I'd add a known-good calibration resistor or voltage reference on the board so the system can perform a self-check during startup.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle drift over temperature and time? What ADC resolution would you target and why?

## Q4: A design review reveals that your PCB has a potential ground loop between the analog sensor section and the digital processing section. How would you analyze and resolve this?
**Answer:** First, I'd identify the current paths that create the loop. A ground loop typically forms when there are multiple return paths for a signal, often through a combination of PCB traces, cable shields, and chassis connections. In a mixed-signal design, the classic problem is that digital return currents (which are noisy and have fast edges) share a portion of the analog ground path, injecting noise into the sensitive analog circuitry. I'd analyze the layout by looking at where the analog and digital grounds connect — ideally they should meet at a single point (star ground) near the power supply return. If the layout has a solid ground plane, I'd check whether the plane is split under the analog section and whether any traces cross the split. A better approach for modern mixed-signal designs is to use a single continuous ground plane but physically partition the board so that analog components and their return currents are confined to one area, and digital components to another. The ADC or codec data sheet usually specifies the recommended grounding scheme — I'd follow that exactly. If a loop exists through an external cable (e.g., a sensor cable shield connected at both ends), I'd break the loop by connecting the shield at only one end, typically the receiving end.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the ground loop is caused by the chassis ground connection required for medical safety (IEC 60601)? How would you balance safety grounding against noise performance?

## Q5: (Behavioral) Imagine you're in a design review and a colleague strongly disagrees with your component selection for a critical power rail, arguing that their preferred part is more reliable. How would you handle the disagreement?
**Answer:** I'd treat this as a technical discussion rather than a personal disagreement. First, I'd ask my colleague to walk me through their concerns and the specific data behind their recommendation — is it based on a known failure mode, a field return history, or a derating guideline? I'd then share my own selection criteria: the part's electrical performance in this application (efficiency, ripple, transient response), its availability and lead time (critical for medical devices where supply chain disruptions can delay regulatory submissions), and its qualification status (AEC-Q100 or medical-grade rating). I'd suggest we compare both parts side-by-side on a whiteboard: list the key parameters, derating margins, and any reliability data we have. If the disagreement persists, I'd propose a practical path forward — perhaps we prototype both options and run accelerated life testing on the critical parameters, or consult the component manufacturer's field application engineer for an independent assessment. In a medical device context, the decision ultimately needs to be documented in the design history file with a rationale, so I'd make sure we capture both viewpoints and the agreed-upon resolution. The goal is to reach the best technical decision for patient safety, not to "win" the argument.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if your colleague is more senior and pulls rank? How would you escalate without damaging the working relationship?
