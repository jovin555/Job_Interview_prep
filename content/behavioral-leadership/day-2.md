# behavioral-leadership — Day 2

## Q1: How would you approach leading a cross-functional root-cause investigation when a critical issue is discovered during regulatory compliance testing of a medical device?

**Answer:** I would structure the investigation using a formal 8D or similar methodology to ensure it's thorough, documented, and defensible for regulatory purposes. The first step is to immediately contain the issue — for example, quarantining affected units and assessing whether the testing gap affects previously released devices. Then I'd assemble a small cross-functional team including design engineering, quality, regulatory, and manufacturing representatives. I'd establish clear roles: a team lead (likely myself), a facilitator for the investigation process, and subject matter experts for each domain. We'd systematically define the problem with measurable criteria, perform a root cause analysis using tools like fishbone diagrams and 5-whys, and validate each potential root cause with data rather than assumptions. Throughout, I'd maintain a living document that captures every decision, test result, and rationale — this becomes part of the design history file. The key leadership challenge is keeping the team focused on finding the true root cause rather than jumping to quick fixes, while also managing schedule pressure from program management. I'd hold brief daily stand-ups to track progress and remove blockers, and escalate resource needs early if the investigation reveals deeper systemic issues.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle disagreement within the team about the likely root cause? What would you do if the investigation starts pointing toward a design decision you personally championed?

## Q2: How would you handle a situation where a junior engineer on your team consistently delivers PCB layouts that pass electrical checks but have poor manufacturability — for example, tight component spacing that causes assembly yield issues?

**Answer:** I'd approach this as a coaching opportunity rather than a performance problem. First, I'd review the specific layouts together with the engineer, pointing out the manufacturability issues and explaining the downstream consequences — higher scrap rates, longer assembly times, and potential reliability concerns from difficult soldering. I'd share concrete examples from past projects where similar layout choices caused problems, and walk through the design rules and guidelines that address those issues (such as minimum component spacing, keep-out zones for pick-and-place nozzles, and thermal relief requirements). I'd then establish a structured review process: before submitting a layout for final approval, the engineer would run a manufacturability checklist I'd provide, and we'd do a brief design review together. Over time, I'd gradually reduce oversight as their judgment improves. If the pattern persists despite coaching, I'd dig deeper to understand whether it's a knowledge gap, a tool limitation, or a misunderstanding of priorities — and adjust my approach accordingly. The goal is to build their capability while maintaining quality standards for the product.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the engineer pushes back and argues their approach is actually better for signal integrity? How would you balance manufacturability with electrical performance requirements?

## Q3: How would you approach prioritizing competing technical work across multiple medical device projects when you're the only senior hardware engineer on the team?

**Answer:** I'd start by getting clarity from program management and project leads on the criticality and timeline for each project's upcoming milestones — particularly regulatory submission dates, clinical trial deadlines, and manufacturing ramp-up schedules. Medical devices have hard regulatory gates that can't slip without significant business impact, so those typically take priority. I'd then map out the technical dependencies: which tasks require my specific expertise versus tasks a more junior engineer could handle with guidance. I'd look for opportunities to parallelize work — for example, if one project needs a schematic review and another needs test plan development, I might do the schematic review in a focused block while a junior engineer drafts the test plan under my direction. I'd also be transparent with stakeholders about capacity: if I'm genuinely overcommitted, I'd present the trade-offs clearly — "I can deliver X by Friday, but Y will slip to next week unless we reprioritize" — and let them make the call. Finally, I'd protect focused engineering time by batching meetings and setting aside uninterrupted blocks for deep technical work, while keeping a visible task board so everyone can see current priorities and progress.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle a situation where two project leads both insist their work is the highest priority? What criteria would you use to escalate?

## Q4: How would you approach mentoring a team of engineers who are transitioning from industrial or consumer electronics to medical device development, particularly around the mindset shift required for IEC 60601 compliance?

**Answer:** I'd start by acknowledging that the transition involves a significant mindset shift — from "make it work" to "make it provably safe and reliable under all foreseeable conditions." I'd organize a series of focused learning sessions covering the key differences: the concept of means of patient protection (MOPP) versus means of operator protection (MOOP), creepage and clearance requirements, leakage current limits, and the importance of single-fault condition analysis. Rather than just lecturing, I'd use real examples from past projects (anonymized) to illustrate what happens when these requirements are missed — for instance, a design that passed functional tests but failed dielectric strength testing because of inadequate insulation spacing. I'd also integrate compliance thinking into our regular design reviews by adding specific checklist items: "What are the isolation barriers in this design? How would we test for single-fault conditions? What's the worst-case leakage current path?" Over time, I'd encourage the team to develop the habit of asking "what if this component fails short?" as a routine part of their design process. The cultural shift is the hardest part — moving from optimizing for cost or performance to optimizing for patient safety — so I'd reinforce that mindset by celebrating compliance catches during reviews rather than treating them as problems.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle an experienced engineer who resists the additional documentation burden of medical device development? What's your approach to making compliance feel like engineering excellence rather than bureaucratic overhead?

## Q5: How would you approach leading a design review for a complex mixed-signal PCB when you know there are strong disagreements between the analog and digital engineers on the team about the grounding strategy?

**Answer:** I'd structure the review to first establish shared facts before debating opinions. I'd start by asking each engineer to present their proposed grounding approach with specific technical justifications — not just "this is how we always do it," but concrete reasoning tied to the circuit's requirements: noise sensitivity of the analog front-end, return current paths for high-speed digital signals, and the specific frequencies involved. I'd then facilitate a comparison against established best practices for mixed-signal grounding, such as using a solid ground plane with careful partitioning rather than physically splitting the plane, which often causes more problems than it solves. If the disagreement persists, I'd propose a data-driven resolution: we identify a critical performance metric (like ADC noise floor or EMI emissions at a specific frequency), build a small test board or simulation model for each approach, and measure the results. This turns a subjective debate into an objective engineering decision. Throughout, my role is to keep the discussion respectful and focused on technical merits, not personalities — and to ensure we capture the rationale for whatever decision we make in the design history file, since grounding choices can have regulatory implications for emissions and immunity testing.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the disagreement is causing schedule delays and you need to make a decision without test data? How would you decide which approach to go with?
