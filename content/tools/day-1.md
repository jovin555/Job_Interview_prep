# tools — Day 1

## Q1: How would you approach selecting the right PCB design tool for a new medical device project?

**Answer:** The selection depends on several factors beyond just feature lists. First, I'd assess the project's complexity—does it require high-speed design capabilities, differential pair routing, impedance control, or multi-layer stackup management? For a medical device, regulatory documentation is critical, so the tool must support design rule checking (DRC) that can be tied to your company's design standards, and ideally generate output files that integrate with your document control system. I'd also consider team experience: if the team is already proficient in a particular EDA tool, switching carries a productivity cost. For smaller teams or early-stage projects, tools with lower licensing overhead and good community support can be practical. For larger, regulated programs, enterprise tools with simulation integration, version control, and library management features often justify their cost. Finally, I'd evaluate the tool's ability to export to common manufacturing formats (Gerber, ODB++, IPC-2581) and whether it supports the simulation tools (SPICE, SI/PI analysis) you plan to use downstream.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle migrating a design from one EDA tool to another mid-project? What library management practices would you put in place to ensure consistency across tools?

## Q2: When debugging a firmware-hardware interaction issue on a prototype, what is your systematic approach to isolating the root cause?

**Answer:** I start by clearly defining the symptom and the conditions under which it occurs—does it happen consistently, intermittently, or only under specific loads or temperatures? Then I separate the problem into domains: is it likely a hardware issue (power supply noise, signal integrity, component tolerance) or a firmware issue (timing, register configuration, interrupt handling)? A practical first step is to verify power rails with an oscilloscope—many intermittent issues trace back to supply ripple or droop under load. Next, I check the communication bus (I2C, SPI, etc.) with a logic analyzer or scope to confirm signal levels, timing, and protocol compliance. On the firmware side, I add targeted debug output (over UART or GPIO toggling) to confirm code execution paths and timing. I use a methodical divide-and-conquer: disable non-essential subsystems, simplify the firmware to a minimal test case, and swap known-good components if possible. Throughout, I document each test and its result so I can track what has been ruled out. Tools like a multimeter, oscilloscope, logic analyzer, and a JTAG/SWD debugger are essential—but the most important tool is a structured, hypothesis-driven mindset.

**Possible follow-ups:** What would you do if the issue only appears after the device has been running for several hours? How do you decide when to involve a colleague versus continuing to debug alone?

## Q3: How would you approach setting up a version control and collaboration workflow for a mixed hardware-firmware project?

**Answer:** For hardware, I'd use a version control system that can handle binary files efficiently—Git with Git LFS is common, though some teams prefer Subversion for large binary PCB files. The key is to establish a clear branching strategy: a main branch for released/approved designs, development branches for active work, and feature branches for experimental changes. For firmware, standard Git workflows apply, but the hardware and firmware repositories should be linked—either through a common repository with submodules, or through a project management tool that tracks which firmware version matches which hardware revision. I'd enforce commit messages that reference design decisions or issue tracker IDs. For PCB design files, I'd also maintain a separate "released" archive (outside the active development repo) that is read-only and backed up, because regulatory audits require immutable records of what was manufactured. Collaboration tools like Altium 365 or similar cloud-based EDA platforms can help with real-time co-design, but the fundamental discipline of regular commits, code reviews (for firmware), and design reviews (for hardware) is what makes the workflow reliable.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle a situation where a hardware change requires a firmware change, and both are being developed in parallel? What do you do if a team member accidentally commits a large binary file that bloats the repository?

## Q4: You are debugging a prototype that intermittently resets. Walk through how you would use an oscilloscope and logic analyzer to isolate the cause.

**Answer:** I'd start with the oscilloscope on the main power rail, set to trigger on a negative-going edge below the nominal voltage—this catches brown-out resets. I'd use a long timebase to look for periodic dips that might correlate with a specific subsystem turning on (e.g., a motor, radio, or heater). If the power rail is clean, I'd move to the reset pin of the microcontroller, triggering on any glitch or low pulse. Many resets come from an external watchdog or voltage supervisor, so I'd check those outputs too. Next, I'd use the logic analyzer to capture the reset pin alongside critical signals: the watchdog timer's output, any enable pins on power regulators, and the microcontroller's status pins. I'd set the analyzer to trigger on the reset event and look backward in time to see what happened just before—was there an I2C bus lockup, a glitch on an interrupt line, or a sequence of events that overloaded the supply? If the reset is very rare, I might leave the logic analyzer running in deep memory mode overnight, or use a microcontroller with built-in reset-cause registers to log the source of the last reset. The combination of analog (scope) and digital (logic analyzer) views is powerful because some resets are caused by noise on a signal line that looks clean on a logic analyzer but shows ringing on a scope.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the reset only happens when the device is connected to a specific external cable or accessory? How would you use a differential probe versus a single-ended probe in this scenario?

## Q5: (Behavioral) Imagine you are leading a design review for a complex PCB, and a junior engineer strongly disagrees with your component placement strategy, arguing for a different layout. How would you handle the disagreement?

**Answer:** I would first acknowledge their perspective and thank them for raising the concern—good design reviews depend on everyone feeling safe to challenge decisions. I'd ask them to walk me through their reasoning: what specific problem do they see with my placement, and what advantage does their alternative offer? This helps me understand whether they've identified a genuine issue I missed, or whether they're operating from incomplete information. I'd then explain my rationale, referencing specific constraints: thermal management, signal integrity requirements, manufacturing panelization rules, or regulatory creepage/clearance distances. If the disagreement persists, I'd suggest we prototype both approaches in the EDA tool and run a quick simulation or DRC comparison—data often resolves opinion-based disputes. If time is tight, I might propose a compromise: use my placement for the critical high-speed or high-power section, but try their approach for a less critical area where the risk is lower. Ultimately, as the lead, I own the decision, but I'd make sure the junior engineer understands why the final choice was made and feels heard. After the review, I might also mentor them on the specific constraints they hadn't considered, turning the disagreement into a learning opportunity.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the junior engineer's manager (not you) later questions your decision? How would you ensure the team maintains a culture where junior members feel comfortable challenging senior engineers?
