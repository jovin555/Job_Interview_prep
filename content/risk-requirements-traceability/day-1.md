# risk-requirements-traceability — Day 1

## Q1: How would you approach establishing a requirements traceability framework for a new medical device development project?
**Answer:** I would start by defining the traceability strategy during the planning phase, before any detailed design begins. The framework needs to connect three layers: user/clinical needs, system requirements, and design/verification artifacts. I'd use a requirements management tool (like DOORS or Jama) to create a structured hierarchy. Each system requirement should be uniquely identified and linked upward to its originating stakeholder or clinical need, and downward to the specific design elements, test cases, and risk controls that satisfy it. For medical devices, I'd also ensure bidirectional traceability to the risk management file per ISO 14971 — every hazardous situation and associated risk control measure should trace to a requirement and its verification. The key is establishing the schema early, training the team on consistent linking conventions, and enforcing traceability as part of design reviews rather than retrofitting it at the end.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle a situation where a requirement changes mid-project — what does the traceability update process look like? What level of granularity is appropriate for trace links (per paragraph, per sentence, per test case)?

## Q2: What is the relationship between ISO 14971 risk management and requirements traceability in a medical device context?
**Answer:** ISO 14971 requires that risks are identified, analyzed, evaluated, and controlled, and that the effectiveness of risk controls is verified. Requirements traceability is the mechanism that makes this verifiable and auditable. Each risk control measure identified during the risk management process should map to one or more system or design requirements — for example, a requirement specifying a software timeout function might be the implementation of a risk control for "device fails to respond." That requirement then traces to a verification test that confirms the timeout works correctly. Conversely, every safety-critical requirement should be traceable back to a risk analysis entry that justifies why it exists. This bidirectional linking ensures that no risk control is implemented without verification, and no safety requirement exists without a documented risk rationale. In practice, I'd include a "Risk Control ID" field in the requirements database and a "Related Requirements" field in the risk management file to maintain this linkage.

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you handle a risk control that is implemented across multiple subsystems — how do you maintain traceability in that case? What do you do when a verification test fails for a risk control requirement?

## Q3: How would you structure a Design Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (DFMEA) to ensure it integrates well with requirements traceability?
**Answer:** I would structure the DFMEA so that each failure mode is explicitly linked to the specific requirement it could violate. The DFMEA header should include a column for "Related Requirement ID" so that when a failure mode is identified, the team immediately knows which system or design requirement is at risk. Conversely, the requirements database should have a field or tag indicating whether a requirement has associated DFMEA entries. This creates a closed loop: requirements inform what could fail, and DFMEA findings can drive new or modified requirements. I'd also ensure that the DFMEA's "Current Controls" column maps to verification activities that already exist in the test plan — not separate, unlinked activities. During design reviews, I'd review the DFMEA alongside the requirements traceability matrix to check for gaps: requirements with no associated failure modes (possibly unnecessary requirements) and failure modes with no linked requirements (possibly missing requirements).

**Possible follow-ups:** How do you prioritize which failure modes to address first when resources are limited? How would you handle a DFMEA finding that suggests a requirement is impossible to verify?

## Q4: A design review reveals that several system requirements have no downward traceability to design elements or test cases. How would you handle this?
**Answer:** First, I'd assess whether these are truly untraced or simply missing from the traceability matrix due to documentation gaps. I'd convene a quick session with the design and test leads to walk through each untraced requirement and determine: (1) Is there a design element that implements this requirement but wasn't linked? (2) Is there a test case that verifies it but wasn't linked? (3) Or is the requirement actually unimplemented or unverified? For cases (1) and (2), I'd add the missing links and document the correction in the traceability matrix. For case (3), this is a more serious finding — it means either the requirement is not actually needed (and should be removed or re-scoped) or there's a gap in design or test coverage that needs a corrective action. I'd flag this for the project manager and systems engineer to decide whether to add the missing design work or formally descope the requirement. I'd also update the risk management file if the requirement was linked to a risk control. Finally, I'd use this as a process improvement opportunity — why were these links missed? Was the traceability process unclear, or was it not enforced during earlier reviews?

**Possible follow-ups:** How would you communicate this finding to a regulatory auditor if it's discovered during an audit? What metrics would you track to prevent this from recurring?

## Q5: (Behavioral) Imagine you're leading a cross-functional team where the systems engineer insists on very granular requirements (every paragraph a separate requirement), while the firmware lead argues this creates excessive overhead and wants broader, more functional requirements. How would you resolve this disagreement?
**Answer:** I'd start by acknowledging that both perspectives have merit — granularity aids traceability and test coverage, but excessive granularity can create maintenance burden and slow development. I'd facilitate a structured discussion focused on the project's risk profile and regulatory context. For a Class II medical device, I'd propose a tiered approach: critical safety and risk-control requirements get fine granularity with unique IDs and full traceability, while non-safety functional requirements can be written at a higher level, perhaps grouped by feature or subsystem. I'd also suggest a pilot: take one subsystem and write requirements both ways, then evaluate the effort to maintain traceability and the clarity for design and test teams. The goal is to find a pragmatic middle ground that satisfies regulatory expectations without paralyzing development. If we can't agree, I'd escalate to the project's risk management file — requirements that trace to risk controls must be granular enough to verify the control's effectiveness; others can be more flexible. Ultimately, I'd document the agreed convention in the project's requirements management plan so everyone has a clear reference.

**Possible follow-ups:** What if the disagreement is not about granularity but about whether a particular requirement should exist at all? How would you handle a team member who refuses to follow the agreed traceability convention?
